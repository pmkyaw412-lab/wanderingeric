title: Reading a weld by its colors
date: 2026-06-28
description: The heat-tint on cooled steel is a temperature map. Here's how to read it.
---

Run a bead on clean steel and let it cool, and the metal writes down what happened to it. A band of colors blooms outward from the weld — pale straw, then bronze, then a deep mulberry, and finally that unmistakable temper blue before the color fades back into bare grey.

None of it is pigment. It's a film of oxide so thin that light interferes with itself passing through it, the same physics as oil on a puddle. The hotter the steel got, the thicker the film grew, and the further along the spectrum the color lands. Which means the rainbow around a weld isn't decoration — it's a temperature map you can read at a glance.

Welders read it without thinking. A tight, narrow band means the heat stayed where it should. A wide, blown-out wash of color means the surrounding metal soaked up more energy than it ought to have, and its properties may have quietly changed.

I picked this palette for the site for a simple reason: it's the most honest color scheme I know. The steel isn't trying to look like anything. It's just telling you what it went through.
